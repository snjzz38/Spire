Extracurriculars

Beyond your grades and test scores, your extracurricular activities paint a picture of who you are, what you're passionate about, and how you'll contribute to a university community.

Extracurriculars Ranked by Prestige (Tiers)

Admissions officers often think about extracurriculars in terms of tiers of achievement and impact. While there's no official system, this is a helpful way to understand what makes an activity stand out.

Tier 1: Exceptional and Rare Achievements

These activities are exceptionally rare for a high school student and demonstrate a level of talent or initiative that is nationally or even internationally recognized. They are significant differentiators in highly selective admissions.

  • National/International Recognition: Winning a prestigious national competition (e.g., Regeneron Science Talent Search, a major Olympiad) or receiving a significant award for your work.
  • Exceptional Talent: Being a highly recruited athlete for a D1 sport, a published author, or a musician who has performed at a renowned venue like Carnegie Hall.
  • Major Initiative: Founding a successful non-profit or business that has a measurable, large-scale impact and has gained significant media attention.

Examples: Placing as a finalist in the Intel ISEF, being named a U.S. Presidential Scholar, or developing a widely used mobile application with thousands of downloads.

Tier 2: High-Level Achievement and Leadership

While more common than Tier 1, these activities still show a high degree of achievement, leadership, and dedication. They are highly respected and form the core of a very strong application.

  • State or Regional Recognition: Achieving all-state honors in music or sports, winning a major state-level academic competition, or having your research published in a regional journal.
  • Significant Leadership Roles: Serving as the president of a large, active club (like Student Government or Model UN) or founding a successful new club that has a major impact on your school community.
  • Selective Programs: Being selected for a highly competitive and prestigious summer program or a competitive research internship.

Examples: Being the captain of a state-championship-winning team, serving as the editor-in-chief of the school newspaper, or attending a program like the Research Science Institute (RSI).

Tier 3: Active Participation and Minor Leadership

This tier represents meaningful and consistent involvement. It shows you are a reliable and engaged member of your community, which is a strong indicator of how you might participate in campus life.

  • Consistent Commitment: Being a dedicated member of a sports team, club, or volunteer organization for multiple years.
  • Minor Leadership Roles: Taking on responsibility, such as being a club treasurer, a section leader in the band, or the captain of the junior varsity team.
  • Substantive Contribution: Actively contributing to a group's goals, even without a formal title, like organizing events or managing a project.

Examples: Being a dedicated member of the debate team for three years, consistently volunteering at a local animal shelter, or holding the position of secretary in the student council.

Tier 4: General Membership and Participation

These activities are the most common and represent the starting point of engagement. They are valuable for showing that you are willing to explore different interests and be part of a group.

  • General Membership: Being a member of several clubs without a specific leadership position or major responsibilities.
  • Sporadic or Short-Term Involvement: Volunteering for a one-time event or joining a club for a single semester.
  • Exploring Interests: Using activities to show a breadth of interests and a willingness to try new things.

Examples: Being a general member of the French club, participating in a school-wide charity drive for a week, or attending meetings for the environmental club.

Finding Your Profile: Spike, Well-Rounded, or Interdisciplinary?

There are different strategies for building your extracurricular profile. Understanding these can help you decide where to focus your energy.

A "spike" refers to developing a deep and impressive expertise in one particular area. Instead of participating in many different activities, you focus your time and energy on one or two passions. Highly selective universities often look for students with spikes to create a diverse class of specialists.

The well-rounded applicant participates in a variety of activities, demonstrating a broad range of interests and abilities. The key is to show leadership and commitment across your activities to avoid looking like a "jack of all trades, but a master of none."

ü§ù The Interdisciplinary Profile

An interdisciplinary approach involves combining interests from different fields to create a unique niche. For example, a student passionate about both computer science and art could create a project on digital art. This shows creativity and innovation.

Ultimately, the most compelling extracurricular profile is one that is authentic to you. Your activities should tell a story about your passions, your commitment, and the unique qualities you'll bring to a college campus.

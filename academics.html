Academics

When you start thinking about university, you'll hear a lot about grades, GPAs, and standardized tests. They're the first thing admissions officers look at to see if you're a good fit.

Grades and What They Mean

Your grades are a primary way for universities to judge your academic potential. While a lot of people just think of them as a number, there's a lot more to it.

üá∫üá∏ GPA (US System)

In the United States, your grades are typically converted into a Grade Point Average (GPA). Universities often recalculate this GPA based on the difficulty of your classes and the context of your school. Essentially, if you take an easier course and get 95%, then it can be weighted less compared to a harder course where you got 92%.

üá®üá¶ Percentage Grades (Canada & others)

In many countries, like Canada, universities use a percentage-based grading system. They often have an "adjustment factor" for different high schools. This helps them balance out a percentage from a school known for giving high marks versus a percentage from a school that is notoriously difficult.

üåç International Systems

Beyond these, there are many other systems. For example, the International Baccalaureate (IB) program uses a 7-point scale for its classes. Other European and Asian countries use their own unique systems. Each university is familiar with these different scales and knows how to evaluate them fairly.

Remember: Your grades are not just for university applications. A good GPA or percentage is essential when applying to jobs, when you are a college student.

The Importance of Course Rigor

Simply having good grades isn't always enough; universities also want to see that you challenged yourself. This is what's known as "course rigor." Taking more difficult classes shows that you're not afraid of a challenge and are ready for university-level work.

  • Advanced Placement (AP) These are college-level courses offered in high school. If you score well on the final exam, you might even earn college credit. For more on AP, check out the official information from the College Board. Learn more about the AP Program
  • International Baccalaureate (IB) The IB Diploma Programme is a comprehensive and challenging two-year curriculum that is widely recognized internationally. It's a great option for students looking for a broad, interdisciplinary education. Find out about the IB Diploma Programme
  • Gifted & Honors Programs Many schools offer specific programs for gifted or high-achieving students. These programs vary widely, but they typically offer a more rigorous curriculum. Ask your school counselor for details on any gifted or honors programs available at your school.

The Role of Standardized Tests

While academics at your school are important, standardized tests provide universities with a common baseline to compare students from different schools and regions.

Major Standardized Tests

For US Universities

The SAT and ACT are the two most common standardized tests for US university applications. You should refer to the webpage we have on this already for more information about them.

For International Universities

Many countries have their own national entrance exams that are a major factor in university admissions. These tests can be very competitive and are essential for certain universities. See a list of global university entrance exams

In the end, it's all about finding a balance. While grades and test scores are crucial, they are just one part of your story. A strong academic record combined with a genuine passion for learning and a willingness to challenge yourself will set you up for success.

SAT & ACT: Your Guide to U.S. College Entrance Exams

Everything Canadian students need to know about standardized testing for U.S. university admissions

What Are the SAT and ACT?

The SAT and ACT are standardized tests required by most U.S. universities for admission. Think of them as the American equivalent to final exams, but designed to measure college readiness rather than specific course content.

Key Differences at a Glance:

  • SAT: 3 hours, focuses on Evidence-Based Reading, Writing, and Math
  • ACT: 2 hours 55 minutes, includes English, Math, Reading, Science, plus optional Writing

Do I Need to Take These as a Canadian Student?

Short answer: Yes, if you're applying to U.S. colleges.

Most U.S. universities require either the SAT or ACT from international students, including Canadians. However, some schools have become test-optional, especially post-COVID.

Quick tip: Check each university's specific requirements on their admissions page, as policies vary widely.

Which Test Should I Choose?

Take a practice test for both to see which suits your strengths better. Here's a quick comparison:

Choose the SAT if you:

  • Prefer longer time per question
  • Excel at reading comprehension and vocabulary
  • Like math problems that focus on algebra and data analysis

Choose the ACT if you:

  • Work well under time pressure
  • Are strong in science reasoning
  • Prefer more straightforward, fact-based questions

Free Practice Tests:

  • Official SAT Practice (Khan Academy)
  • Official ACT Practice Tests

Test Dates and Registration

SAT

  • Offered: 7 times per year (March, May, June, August, October, November, December)
  • Registration: College Board website
  • Cost: $60 USD (plus international fee for Canadian students)

ACT

  • Offered: 6 times per year (February, April, June, July, September, December)
  • Registration: ACT website
  • Cost: $63 USD (plus international fee)

Important: Canadian test centers are available in major cities. Register 6-8 weeks early as spots fill quickly.

When Should I Take These Tests?

Recommended Timeline:

  • Grade 11 (Junior Year): Take your first attempt in spring (March/April), but the earlier, the better; just keep watch of costs
  • Grade 12 (Senior Year): Retake in fall if needed (August/September/October)

Why this timing works:

  • You'll have covered most necessary math concepts by Grade 11
  • Gives you time to retake if your first score isn't where you want it
  • Meets early application deadlines (November 1st for many schools)

In-Person vs. Digital Testing

Digital SAT (launched 2024)

  • Shorter test time (2 hours 14 minutes vs. 3 hours)
  • Adaptive testing (questions adjust based on your performance)
  • Available on weekends at test centers
  • Calculator allowed for entire math section

Paper ACT (still available)

  • Traditional format most students are familiar with
  • No adaptive elements
  • Consistent timing throughout
  • Standard pencil-and-paper format

Score Ranges and What's Considered Good

Test Score Range Breakdown
SAT 400-1600 total • 200-800 Evidence-Based Reading and Writing
• 200-800 Math
ACT 1-36 composite score Average of four subject scores (English, Math, Reading, Science)

Target Scores for Canadian Students:

  • Competitive schools: SAT 1400+ / ACT 30+
  • Top-tier schools: SAT 1500+ / ACT 33+
  • Ivy League/Elite: SAT 1550+ / ACT 35+

Tip: Check average scores for your target schools to set realistic goals.

Test Day Essentials

What to Bring:

  • Valid photo ID (passport recommended for international students)
  • Admission ticket
  • No. 2 pencils (for paper tests) or ensure your device is charged (for digital)
  • Approved calculator for math sections

What NOT to bring:

  • Phone (must be turned off and stored)
  • Smart watches
  • Food or drinks (provided break snacks only)
  • Unauthorized electronic devices

After You Get Your Scores

Score Reporting:

  • SAT scores available in 2-3 weeks
  • ACT scores available in 3-8 weeks
  • Free score reports to 4 colleges included in registration fee

Superscoring: Many schools accept "superscores" - your highest section scores across multiple test dates. This means retaking can only help, not hurt.

Next Steps

  1. Take a practice test to determine which exam suits you better
  2. Register early for your preferred test date
  3. Create a prep timeline using the free resources above
  4. Research your target schools' average scores and requirements
  5. Consider retaking if your first score doesn't meet your goals
